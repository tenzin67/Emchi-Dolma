## A Language Written in Your Blood

Imagine a physician who can place three fingers gently on your wrist — and from that single point of contact, read the state of your liver, kidneys, heart, lungs, spleen, and more. Who can detect patterns of imbalance months or even years before they become serious illness. Who can distinguish between twelve distinct organ systems through the subtle whisper of your pulse.

This is not mysticism. This is **rtsa brtag** (རྩ་བརྟགས་) — Tibetan pulse diagnosis — one of the most sophisticated and refined diagnostic arts in the history of human medicine.

## The Foundations of Pulse Diagnosis

In Tibetan Medicine, the pulse is understood as a direct window into the functioning of all organ systems. The heart pumps blood throughout the body, and that blood carries information — about the state of each organ, the balance of the three Nyepas, the quality of vital energies, and the presence of imbalance or disease.

A trained Tibetan physician learns to read this information through years of dedicated practice, cultivating sensitivity in the fingertips that allows them to perceive subtle qualities most people would never notice.

## The Three Positions, Six Organs

The pulse is read at three positions on each wrist, using the index, middle, and ring fingers. Each position corresponds to specific organ pairs:

### Right Wrist
| Finger | Superficial | Deep |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Index  | Lungs       | Large intestine |
| Middle | Stomach     | Spleen |
| Ring   | Heart protector | Triple heater |

### Left Wrist
| Finger | Superficial | Deep |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Index  | Heart       | Small intestine |
| Middle | Liver       | Gallbladder |
| Ring   | Kidney (L)  | Bladder |

*(Note: Traditional pulse positions may vary by lineage and school of teaching.)*

## Qualities the Physician Reads

Within each position, the physician assesses numerous qualities:

**Rate**: How fast or slow is the pulse? (Reflects hot vs. cold conditions)

**Strength**: Is it strong and forceful, or weak and faint? (Reflects vitality and depletion)

**Depth**: Is it felt near the surface or only with deep pressure? (Reflects the nature of imbalance)

**Rhythm**: Is it regular, intermittent, or erratic? (Reflects heart and systemic health)

**Width**: Is it broad or thin like a thread? (Reflects fluid balance and constitution)

**Texture**: Is it hard, soft, slippery, rough? (Reflects the specific nature of imbalance)

**Character**: Does it remind you of a frog jumping, a crow pecking, a fish darting? Tibetan medical texts use vivid natural metaphors to describe pulse qualities — a testament to the tradition's grounding in careful observation of the living world.

## Seasonal and Daily Timing

Tibetan pulse reading is also sensitive to time. Different organ pulses are strongest at different hours of the day, following natural rhythms of energy flow through the body. Spring and summer pulse qualities differ from autumn and winter. Even the physician's own state of mind and health can influence reading accuracy — which is why Tibetan physicians traditionally meditate and eat lightly before performing pulse diagnosis.

When you arrive for a pulse reading with Emchi Tsundu Dolma, she will:

1. **Ask about your current symptoms and health history** — understanding your story enriches the reading
2. **Observe your overall appearance** — complexion, eyes, tongue, and posture all speak volumes
3. **Take the pulse** — you'll sit comfortably while she places her fingers at your wrists, often reading each side for several minutes
4. **Explain what she finds** — she will share her findings in clear, accessible language and connect them to your symptoms and constitution
5. **Recommend treatment** — whether herbal medicine, external therapy, dietary changes, or a combination approach

The reading is deeply personal and often remarkably accurate. Many patients find that Emchi identifies patterns they themselves hadn't connected — the way chronic anxiety links to poor digestion, or how old grief lives in the lungs.

In an era of scans, tests, and algorithms, there is something profoundly humanizing about a physician who simply sits with you, listens to your blood, and says: *I see you. I understand what's happening. Let's find our way back together.*
